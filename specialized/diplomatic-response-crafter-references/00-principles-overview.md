# Carnegie Principles Overview

Quick reference for all 30 principles organized by their application to diplomatic response crafting.

## Part One: Fundamental Techniques in Handling People

These three principles form the foundation for all diplomatic communication.

| # | Principle | Application to Responses |
|---|-----------|-------------------------|
| 1 | Don't criticize, condemn, or complain | Never counter-attack; criticism puts people on the defensive and makes them justify themselves |
| 2 | Give honest and sincere appreciation | Thank specific contributions (effort, detail, time taken to write) when genuine |
| 3 | Arouse in the other person an eager want | Frame your response around what they want (resolution, acknowledgment, fairness) |

## Part Two: Six Ways to Make People Like You

Building rapport even in tense exchanges.

| # | Principle | Application to Responses |
|---|-----------|-------------------------|
| 1 | Become genuinely interested in other people | Show you read their comment carefully; reference specific points |
| 2 | Smile | Use warm, non-defensive tone (the written equivalent of a smile) |
| 3 | Remember that a person's name is the sweetest sound | Use their name if known; personalize the response |
| 4 | Be a good listener | Paraphrase their concern to show you understood |
| 5 | Talk in terms of the other person's interests | Connect your response to what matters to them |
| 6 | Make the other person feel important, sincerely | Validate their perspective before adding yours |

## Part Three: How to Win People to Your Way of Thinking

Core principles for persuasive, non-confrontational responses.

| # | Principle | Application to Responses |
|---|-----------|-------------------------|
| 1 | The only way to get the best of an argument is to avoid it | Don't try to "win"; seek understanding |
| 2 | Show respect for the other person's opinions; never say "you're wrong" | "Given X, I can see why you'd feel that way..." |
| 3 | If you are wrong, admit it quickly and emphatically | Own mistakes once, clearly, without hedging |
| 4 | Begin in a friendly way | Open with acknowledgment, not defense |
| 5 | Get the other person saying "yes, yes" immediately | Find common ground before differences |
| 6 | Let the other person do a great deal of the talking | Ask clarifying questions; invite their input |
| 7 | Let the other person feel the idea is theirs | "Does this work for you?" over "You must accept..." |
| 8 | Try honestly to see things from the other person's point of view | Paraphrase their concern in their vocabulary |
| 9 | Be sympathetic with the other person's ideas and desires | "I understand why this situation feels frustrating" |
| 10 | Appeal to the nobler motives | Reference shared standards: fairness, quality, community |
| 11 | Dramatize your ideas | Use concrete examples only when they reduce confusion |
| 12 | Throw down a challenge | Rarely applicable; skip for diplomatic responses |

## Part Four: Be a Leader (How to Change People Without Giving Offense)

When you need to correct, redirect, or set boundaries.

| # | Principle | Application to Responses |
|---|-----------|-------------------------|
| 1 | Begin with praise and honest appreciation | Acknowledge something positive before addressing the issue |
| 2 | Call attention to mistakes indirectly | "I wonder if..." rather than "You did X wrong" |
| 3 | Talk about your own mistakes first | "We could have been clearer about..." |
| 4 | Ask questions instead of giving direct orders | "Would it help if...?" |
| 5 | Let the other person save face | Never humiliate; offer graceful exits |
| 6 | Praise every improvement, however slight | Acknowledge when they make a fair point |
| 7 | Give the other person a fine reputation to live up to | "I know you care about fairness..." |
| 8 | Use encouragement; make the fault seem easy to correct | "This is a simple fix..." |
| 9 | Make the other person happy about doing what you suggest | Frame the solution as benefiting them |

## Principle Selection Guide

**For venting/character attacks**: #1 (no counter-attack), #4 (respect opinions), #6 (their viewpoint), #9 (sympathy)

**For factual disagreements**: #1, #2 (respect), #3 (admit if wrong), #8 (their viewpoint)

**For unmet expectations**: #3 (if wrong, admit), #6 (their viewpoint), #3-arouse (frame solution around their want), #8 (their interests)

**For public reputation concerns**: #10 (nobler motives), #8 (their interests), #6 (their viewpoint)

**For demands/ultimatums**: #3-arouse (eager want), #8 (their interests), #6 (their viewpoint), plus clear boundaries
